## Purpose

This record demonstrates the application of the Seam Discipline Declaration Intake Profile to a two-layer comparison where both layers operate at the same consequential boundary — the at-bind moment when an AI action becomes eligible for consequence — but in fundamentally different capacities.

The value of this example is that it shows how the Declaration Intake / companion-layer approach works in practice: adjacent systems can become comparable without being converted, merged, represented as a single stack, or required to adopt each other's framework.

---

## The Two Layers

### VeriSigil AI
*Language authorized by Raheem Larry Babatunde / VeriSigil AI*

VeriSigil AI operates as a runtime enforcement participant at the consequential boundary — producing and sealing an ALLOW/DENY/ESCALATE admissibility ruling before execution occurs. VeriSigil's evidence is generated by the enforcement participant itself and is cryptographically sealed at the moment of decision.

VeriSigil AI's primary function is runtime admissibility enforcement, human-governance escalation, and cryptographic evidence generation at the point of consequential execution.

VeriSigil AI produces cryptographically sealed admissibility and execution evidence within the governed execution environment. Independent external witnessing is a distinct governance function that VeriSigil does not currently claim to perform.

### ASRO — AI Systems Reliability Operator
*Self-declared by James Aull / MagicianzCardstock LLC*

ASRO operates as an independent witness at the consequential boundary — preserving independently reviewable evidence of the governed-state conditions that existed when consequence became possible, from outside the deploying system's trust boundary, without relying on the deploying system's own self-report.

ASRO explicitly does not produce enforcement rulings, intercept or block AI actions, determine admissibility, control execution, or certify that a composed path is authorized or safe.

---

## The Seam

The seam between VeriSigil and ASRO holds because the two layers operate at the same consequential boundary in different capacities — enforcement participant and independent witness — with non-overlapping authority surfaces and explicit non-claims on both sides.

**Timing position:** Both layers operate at-bind. VeriSigil acts by producing and sealing an admissibility ruling. ASRO witnesses by capturing the governed-state conditions from outside the trust boundary.

**Authority surfaces:** VeriSigil's authority surface is the admissibility ruling and its cryptographic seal. ASRO's authority surface is the independently reviewable evidence record. Neither layer claims the other's function.

**Non-claims alignment:** VeriSigil does not currently claim to perform independent external witnessing of governed-state evidence from outside the trust boundary. ASRO does not claim to produce enforcement rulings, determine admissibility, or control execution. The non-claims do not overlap and do not conflict.

---

## Known Divergence Risk

If ASRO's witness infrastructure were co-located with or controlled by the system being witnessed — including VeriSigil — ASRO's independence would be reduced. In any composed VeriSigil/ASRO deployment, ASRO must remain outside VeriSigil's trust boundary for the governed-state evidence to retain its independent reviewability.

This is a named architectural constraint, not a defect. It applies to any composed path involving ASRO and must be preserved explicitly in deployment design.
